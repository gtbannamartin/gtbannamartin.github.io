Where This Began

They Asked:
"How Do We Get Home Safely?"

In 2017, Martin Pieters — then STEM Director at Rainbow PUSH Coalition — challenged his Saturday Academy students to build something real. Not a classroom exercise. Something that mattered to them.

They chose survival. They mapped every shooting in Chicago from May through June 2017, overlaid it on Google Maps, and built a heat map that gave students a safer route home from school. They called it the Stay Safe App. Teenagers. South Side Chicago. Real data. Real stakes.

The app was unveiled at Rainbow PUSH's 46th Annual Convention, covered by the Chicago Sun-Times and ABC7, and recognized by the Cook County Board of Commissioners. The students who built it are now the staff who run DJMP Institute.

"We know it's not a talent deficiency. It's a resource deficiency."

Martin Pieters · ABC7 Chicago · Rainbow PUSH 46th Annual Convention · 2017

That's the conviction behind everything DJMP Institute builds: Black and Brown students on Chicago's South Side have always had the talent. They have only ever lacked the resources.

Since 2021, DJMP Institute has delivered free, high-quality AI and STEM education to 200+ students across Chicagoland — Saturday academies, after-school programs, robotics competitions, AI research placements — entirely founder-funded. Zero grant revenue. The programs ran because the mission was real enough that people showed up anyway.

Now we're ready to build the summer program those students deserve. The curriculum is written. The space is secured. The instructors are credentialed. The only thing missing is the funding to make it run.

Summer 2026 is 10 weeks away. Thirty seats. Thirty students. Six labs. Your contribution is what fills them.

Where These Programs Run

The Overton Hygienic Building — Not a Detail. A Declaration.

DJMP Institute operates from the Overton Hygienic Building in Bronzeville — built in 1922 by Anthony Overton, born into slavery, who turned it into the first building in Chicago to make professional office space available to Black lawyers, doctors, and architects. The building housed the Douglass National Bank — the first nationally chartered African American-owned bank in the United States. A building that stood as the center of Black economic self-determination a century ago is now home to an organization building AI-powered economic infrastructure for the next century. That is not coincidence. That is continuity.
