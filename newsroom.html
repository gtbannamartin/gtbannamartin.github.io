Origin Story  ·  DJMP News  ·  July 2017

Before PathwayAI, There Was the Stay Safe App

Eight years before PathwayAI, the intellectual DNA of DJMP Institute was already taking shape on the South Side of Chicago. Martin Pieters — then STEM Director at Rainbow PUSH Coalition — challenged his Saturday morning youth program participants to do something radical: use publicly available city data to solve a real problem facing their own community. The problem they chose wasn't abstract. It was survival. How do we get home safely?

The result was the Stay Safe App — a shooting heat map built from Chicago city data and Google Maps, designed to help CPS students navigate around areas recently affected by gun violence. The app was led by Jonathan Key, then a biomedical engineer at the Illinois Institute of Technology, who guided the Saturday Academy students through the technical build. It was unveiled at the Rainbow PUSH Coalition's 46th Annual International Convention at the Hilton Chicago and covered by ABC7 Chicago and the Chicago Sun-Times. The app wasn't a class project. It was civic AI before civic AI had a name.

The students who built it were not strangers. Donald Pieters — Martin's son — co-developed the Stay Safe App alongside Krystopher Williams, his fellow program participant. On the other side of that same PUSH Excel lab, Akash Pondicherry — younger brother of DJMP Institute core staff member and first employee Raj Pondicherry — was growing up in the same Saturday sessions, from elementary school through their time together at Kenwood Academy. Akash and Donald were classmates. Two families, one program, one mission — long before DJMP Institute existed on paper.

That lineage is not coincidence. It is continuity. PathwayAI is the Stay Safe App grown up — more powerful, more scalable, and built for 500,000+ residents across 25 cities. But the question it asks is identical to the one those teenagers asked in 2017: How do we use data to protect the people systems have left behind?

Cook County Board of Commissioners, February 2019. Left to right: Toni Preckwinkle — 35th President of the Cook County Board of Commissioners (since 2010), the first Black woman elected to the office; Commissioner Bill Lowry (3rd District); Bruce Petersen — retired engineer, FIRST Robotics Competition mentor for Illinois, and DJMP Institute's resident robotics champion; Martin Pieters — PUSH Excel STEAM Director, addressing the board; Adam Simpkins, 13, Ogden International High School; Akash Pondicherry, 15, Kenwood Academy — younger brother of DJMP Institute core staff member and first employee Raj Pondicherry; and Commissioner Dennis Deer (2nd District) — the program's champion on the board, whose advocacy made this recognition possible. Commissioner Deer passed away in 2023. He is remembered with deep gratitude.
